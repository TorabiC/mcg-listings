Days on Market in Proper Context

The MLS shows 139 days on market for 46 Northfleet. Taken at face value, that number invites the wrong conclusion. Two facts materially change its meaning.

1. This home was listed before it was finished — and is only now truly on the market

46 Northfleet was listed February 20, 2026, while still under construction. For most of the recorded 139 days, buyers could not walk a finished home, and financing-contingent buyers could not close on one. Construction is now complete and the Certificate of Occupancy has been issued by the City of Bella Vista. As a completed, move-in-ready product — the only condition in which most buyers will transact — this home's effective market exposure is just beginning. The comparable listings it is measured against were, in many cases, listed after completion, making their DOM figures an apples-to-oranges comparison.

2. Several comparables have been on the market far longer than their displayed DOM suggests

MLS days-on-market resets when a listing is withdrawn and relisted. Several comparables in these analyses have been listed multiple times, so their displayed DOM understates their true cumulative market time. Even within the visible data, the pattern is clear: 13 Chichester Circle originally listed at $549,000 and closed at $499,500 after 168 displayed days; 6 Marionet Circle came to market at $649,900 and closed at $575,000 after 220 days; and the broader survey includes listings showing 246 and 345 days on a single listing period alone. The honest comparison is not 139 days versus 47 — it is a just-completed home entering a market where extended and repeated listing periods are common for new construction.

One practical note: regardless of cause, consumer portals display accumulated days, and buyers use that figure in negotiation. Perspective on DOM is the right analytical posture; managing its perception is a marketing task, and the completion of construction and issuance of the CO is precisely the kind of event that legitimately resets the conversation with the market.

The Strategic Dimension: Setting the ICF Benchmark

This consideration extends beyond the current transaction. 46 Northfleet is the only new residential ICF (Insulated Concrete Form) construction in Bella Vista. There is no existing comp set for this product type here — which means this sale will create it.

If the intent is to continue building ICF homes in this market, the closing price of this home becomes the reference point for every appraisal, every buyer negotiation, and every lender conversation on the ICF homes that follow. Appraisers will reach for the nearest comparable ICF sale — and for the foreseeable future, this will be the only one. A closing at $255/SF establishes ICF at a modest premium to conventional construction; a closing at $270+/SF establishes it as a distinct premium category, the way the Grimsby sale established that a $300+/SF transaction is possible in this market.

This cuts both ways and deserves honest treatment. Pricing for a fast sale surrenders benchmark value that future projects would inherit. Holding for the benchmark carries genuine carrying costs — taxes, insurance, capital tied up, and portal-displayed market time — with no guaranteed timeline. The right answer depends on the weight you place on the future ICF pipeline relative to the economics of this individual sale. That weighting is a business decision only you can make; the data above is intended to make it a well-informed one.

Path A — Future ICF Builds Are Intended: Sell the Benchmark, Not the House

If the program below proceeds, the Northfleet closing price is worth more than its own margin. The arithmetic: on a 1,400 SF future build, every $10/SF of appraisable comp value is $14,000 of defensible sale price. A Northfleet close at $276/SF rather than $255/SF carries roughly $21/SF of benchmark value — about $29,000 per future home, or $120,000–175,000 across a four-to-six-home program — before counting its effect on appraisals, lender confidence, and buyer negotiations. Underselling this home surrenders that to save weeks now.

The recommended posture under Path A: hold the ask in the $575,000–$590,000 range and treat the Certificate of Occupancy as the re-launch event. For the first time, the home can be photographed and marketed as a finished product — new photography, remarks rewritten around the completed ICF home and immediate occupancy, and a full re-targeting of the market with the new assets across MLS, syndication, and digital channels. The carrying cost of patience — roughly $3,000–3,500 per month in taxes, insurance, utilities, and cost of capital — is partially offset if the market's recent 4% appreciation continues, though that is not assured. Hold with a pre-agreed review point (for example, 60 days post-relaunch) at which Path B pricing takes over if the market has not responded.

If future ICF builds are intended (Path A): hold 46 Northfleet at $575,000–$590,000 and re-launch on the Certificate of Occupancy with finished-product marketing assets; use buyer incentives rather than price reductions to protect the recorded $/SF if encouragement is needed. The benchmark is worth roughly $29,000 per future home and $120,000–175,000 across a program. Redeploy into 1,300–1,400 SF product at $360,000–$390,000, where the size-compression data shows the market has paid its highest $/SF and transacted fastest. Target $265–276/SF supported by the Northfleet comp; break-even is $246/SF at the modeled cost, with $250/SF as the practical floor. Drive cost out of repeatable plans — each $5/SF removed adds roughly five points of margin — and convert spec traffic into zero-market-risk custom contracts. Set a review point (60 days post-relaunch) at which Path B pricing takes over if the premium buyer has not appeared.

If this is a one-time project (Path B): the benchmark has no inheritor, so weight certainty over premium. Option 2 ($539,900, $253/SF) offers the strongest balance — priced inside the proven size-matched ceiling at a level where comparables have historically moved quickly, returning $34,300. Option 1 ($559,900) preserves $18,700 more if the premium buyer materializes; Option 3 ($519,900) prices at the segment core for maximum certainty at a modest 3.3% return. All three exit profitably, and none requires the market to do anything it has not already done — though in the current rate environment, no timeline is guaranteed at any price.

Either way, the pricing floor discipline is identical: below roughly $503,200 this asset sells at a loss, and below $246/SF no future ICF home should be built at the modeled cost. The market data across 169 comparables is unambiguous about where the floors are — the remaining choice is how much of the ICF premium to monetize now versus bank as the benchmark for what comes next.
